This week produced the clearest public hints yet of the mechanism that matters most to AI 2027: models helping to improve AI systems. Moonshot says an early Kimi K3 handled most of its kernel-optimization work, built a small GPU compiler, completed bounded scientific workflows, and executed a 48-hour chip-design run. OpenAI separately described GPT-Red, an automated red-teamer used to generate attacks that made GPT-5.6 more robust. These are concrete research-loop applications, so the automation score rises. They are not, however, independent proof of a sustained lab-wide acceleration in algorithmic progress: Moonshot’s strongest claims are self-reported, K3’s weights and full technical report are still pending, and automated safety testing is narrower than automating frontier model research.

Capabilities and diffusion also moved forward. Third-party evaluations cited by Reuters place Kimi K3 near the frontier in several coding and agentic tests, while Thinking Machines released the 975-billion-parameter, multimodal Inkling under Apache 2.0. Neither result demonstrates robust autonomy across open-ended work, but together they strengthen the case that advanced capability is spreading beyond a small group of closed US labs. That makes the AI race look more compressed than the scenario’s mid-2026 assumption of a roughly six-month Chinese lag.

Compute remains broadly on an aggressive scaling path: Japan and NVIDIA announced a planned 140-megawatt national AI factory with 27,500 Rubin GPUs. The overall signal therefore moves from 43 to 47—closer to the scenario, but only in the “mixed / broadly near” band. The next decisive evidence would be independently reproducible long-horizon R&D results, the promised K3 weights and technical report, or credible lab-level measurements showing that AI assistance is compounding research progress rather than merely increasing token use and researcher throughput.
